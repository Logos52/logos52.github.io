# Opus - Everything You Think You Know About Vietnam Is Wrong — Mark Moyar

## Core Thesis

Historian Mark Moyar argues the dominant Hollywood-and-academia narrative of Vietnam — that the war was unwinnable, immoral, and an inevitable failure — is wrong on multiple counts. The fight against communist expansion was strategically necessary (the dominoes that *didn't* fall — Indonesia, Thailand, Singapore — bought the time for Southeast Asia to solidify against Mao). The war was lost not on the battlefield but in Washington: McNamara's gradual-escalation game theory, Johnson's refusal to make the case to the American public, and Kissinger's diplomatic mismanagement at the end. The communists took crippling casualties almost throughout. The American defeat was institutional, civil-military, and rhetorical — not military.

## Compressed Takeaways

1. **Vietnam was a war against communism, not against Vietnam.** The Chinese communist victory in 1949 reframed Asia; the US response (Korea, French aid, then direct involvement) was the strategic posture, not a colonial holdover.
2. **The domino theory was partly right.** Indonesia, Thailand, Singapore stayed out of communism *because* the US committed in Vietnam. Cambodia and Laos fell. The wider domino set held.
3. **The 1963 US-backed coup against Ngo Dinh Diem was the watershed.** South Vietnam was stabilizing under Diem; the coup threw the government into chaos from which it never fully recovered. The war became necessary because the American intervention destabilized the friendly regime.
4. **McNamara's gradual escalation was based on academic game theory, not history or strategy.** The Joint Chiefs argued for either fighting hard or getting out. McNamara chose the third option — communicate intent via limited force — which the North Vietnamese read as weakness.
5. **The Gulf of Tonkin was real (the first attack) but McNamara deceived Congress about its nature.** The mess fed conspiracy theories for decades; the underlying decision to expand the war was already in motion before the incident.
6. **The Americans won most engagements decisively.** North Vietnamese commanders themselves were deceiving Hanoi about losses because the truth would have been demoralizing. The Tet Offensive in 1968 was an enormous military defeat for the communists — read by US media as the opposite.
7. **Johnson never made the case to the public.** He prioritized Great Society domestic policy and treated the war as a distraction to be managed quietly. The lack of explanation eroded morale at home and in the field.
8. **The South Vietnamese army was improving by the early 70s.** The 1972 North Vietnamese invasion — 14 divisions, far beyond guerrilla operations — was defeated with US air support but mostly South Vietnamese ground forces. South Vietnam fell only after Congress cut aid in 1974.
9. **Kissinger's 1972 peace deal was a strategic blunder.** It allowed North Vietnamese troops to stay inside South Vietnam after the cease-fire — guaranteeing the resumption of war. Kissinger appeared not to grasp the consequence at the time.
10. **The lasting cultural narrative was shaped by the draft-dodging generation that later dominated media and academia.** Anti-war sentiment had to be retroactively justified through "the war was unwinnable / immoral," because most other framings would require admitting moral cowardice.

## The Civil-Military Failure Mode

The most useful structural frame in the conversation. Moyar's case is not that Vietnam was a tactical failure — most battles were won — but that the *civil-military decision system* failed catastrophically.

- **McNamara**: brilliant manager, low strategic intuition, captured by academic game theory. Trusted Lyndon Johnson far past the point where his own analysis told him the war was failing. Stayed quiet rather than resign or speak honestly. Becomes Moyar's clearest example of "smart people doing terrible strategy."
- **Johnson**: politically capable, strategically unserious. Treated the war as a domestic-political risk to be managed rather than a national enterprise to be led. Never sold the war. Set up his own legitimacy crisis when casualties accumulated without explanation.
- **Kissinger**: brilliant diplomat, fatally over-eager for the deal. The 1972 cease-fire allowed enemy troops to remain in friendly territory. Moyar's diagnosis: Kissinger got so attached to the deal-as-achievement that he gave away its substance.
- **The Joint Chiefs**: correct on strategy throughout (fight hard or get out), ignored by civilian leadership operating on a theory of war they had no military expertise to assess.

This is a Red Team object lesson. The military's "raise the assumption check" function was structurally suppressed by civilian decision-makers committed to their preferred theory. The result was a decade of suboptimal force application driven by a wrong model that nobody with veto power was willing to retire.

## The Coup as the Strategic Pivot

Moyar's most counter-intuitive claim, and the one most worth thinking about. The standard narrative has the US dragged inexorably into Vietnam by the failure of the South Vietnamese government to consolidate. Moyar's reading: Diem's government was *successfully consolidating* by 1962-1963; the war effort was going well; communists themselves were despairing.

Then in 1963 the US, persuaded by a propaganda-driven Buddhist crisis (now known to have been amplified by communist information operations), supported a coup that overthrew Diem. The successor governments were unstable. The war effort collapsed almost overnight. The next four years of escalation were repairing damage the US had inflicted on its own ally.

If Moyar is right, this is the single largest strategic error in modern US foreign policy: the deliberate destruction of a winning ally because that ally was politically inconvenient. Worth pairing with similar patterns in later conflicts (Iran 1953, Iraq 2003, Afghanistan 2001-2021).

## Tet 1968 — The Media-Reality Gap

The cleanest example of how a battlefield victory can become a strategic defeat through media coverage:

- **What happened**: North Vietnamese launched simultaneous attacks on cities throughout South Vietnam during a holiday cease-fire, expecting an urban uprising. The uprising never materialized. Communist forces, trapped in cities they didn't know well, took *crippling* losses.
- **How it was reported**: as a stunning communist breakthrough, evidence that years of US optimism had been delusional, proof that the war could not be won.
- **What followed**: Johnson decided not to run for re-election. Public support for the war collapsed. The military's case for escalation was politically dead.

The gap matters because it shows how *information environment* can flip the meaning of military outcomes. The Americans-and-South-Vietnamese version of Tet was correct in the strict military sense and irrelevant to the political and strategic consequences. The communist version was wrong militarily and *won* the political war.

This is the Red Team analog of what Smith argued in the Critical Thinking interview: the character of the narrative — who's telling it, with what framing, to which audience — can dominate the underlying facts. Strategy must include winning the framing battle, not just the kinetic one.

## The Counterfactual Question

Could the US have won? Moyar's qualified yes:

- Don't support the 1963 coup. Diem stabilizes; war remains manageable.
- Cut the Ho Chi Minh Trail by ground invasion of Laos. The North loses its supply line.
- Fight hard early (Joint Chiefs' option) rather than gradual escalation. The North never gets time to build air defenses.
- Don't sign the 1972 deal that left North Vietnamese troops in the South. Or, having signed it, honor the commitment to South Vietnam when the North reinvaded. Or — given Watergate — at least don't cut aid in 1974.

None of these is obviously a winner in retrospect, but each is significantly better than what was done. The aggregate point: defeat was a product of accumulating bad decisions, not an inevitable consequence of fighting a determined nationalist movement.

## Why the Narrative Persists

Moyar's structural explanation for the persistence of the unwinnable-immoral narrative: the generation that dodged the draft (concentrated in the Northeast and upper Midwest, dominantly white-collar, dominantly liberal) needed a moral justification for not having gone. "The war was wrong and I refused to participate" is the necessary self-story. That generation then became the media establishment, the academic establishment, and the cultural establishment. The narrative they needed became the narrative everyone learned.

The veterans, polled afterward, mostly said they were *proud of their service* and that the problem was civilian leadership tying their hands. That voice never became the dominant one because the people holding it were not the ones writing the histories or making the movies.

This is a useful template for thinking about other narrative-vs-facts cases. *Who needed this narrative to be true, and what would they have had to admit if it weren't?*
