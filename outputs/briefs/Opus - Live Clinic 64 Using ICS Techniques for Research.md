# Opus - Live Clinic 64: Using ICS Techniques for Research

## Core Thesis

Research is learning with a different output format and a higher minimum required thinking level. The same ICS hierarchy applies — the same jigsaw metaphor, the same insistence that understanding is a byproduct on the way to somewhere more important. What makes research distinct is not the input type (papers instead of lectures) but the standard: you can pass most of undergrad by occasionally surfacing into higher-order thinking; you cannot do research that way at all. The clinic makes this concrete by tracing exactly where most researchers stall — reading, highlighting, rearranging, and producing superficial work that reflects consumption rather than thought.

## Compressed Takeaways

1. **Research is not a different kind of task.** It is learning under a higher required thinking standard with a structured output attached.
2. **Higher-order thinking for research means always asking "so what?"** Each paper is a jigsaw piece. Your job is the big picture, not the piece.
3. **Overwhelm in research is not a sign something is wrong.** It is the accurate signal of how complex the field actually is. Don't retreat from it — organize it.
4. **Non-linear notes first; linearizing comes later.** The schema must exist before you can write. Writing before the schema produces rearranged confusion.
5. **The evidence hierarchy is real but not final.** Systematic reviews aggregate but can miss perspectives. A low-citation recent paper can outweigh a high-citation older one.
6. **AI in research: same rules as in learning.** Valid for gap-spotting and perspective-checking. Not valid for evaluating importance or building your structure for you.
7. **Writing is a separate skill.** The quality of your schema determines the quality of your thinking. Writing skill determines whether you can communicate it. Both need work; neither substitutes for the other.

## The Jigsaw Model

The operating metaphor for research-level reading: every paper, every source, every conversation with a supervisor is a jigsaw piece. Your job is not to examine each piece carefully and understand it in isolation. Your job is to hold the bigger picture clearly enough that you can see where the piece fits, where it agrees or disagrees with what you already know, and — more importantly in emerging fields — what the picture reveals is missing.

This is the shift most early researchers fail to make. They read to understand. They should be reading to place. Understanding is a byproduct of that placement process, not the target. The cognitive mode is fundamentally different: instead of asking "what is this saying?" you ask "where does this sit? what does it change? what gap does it fill or expose?"

The consequence of staying at the lower-order level is not just inefficiency. It is a specific kind of blindness — you accumulate papers without building a usable model, and after months of reading you realize you cannot articulate a coherent argument because you have a collection of understood pieces, not an organized picture.

## The Processing Sequence

Sung describes the research processing workflow in three stages, corresponding roughly to the ICS growth arc:

**Stage 1: Non-linear note-taking.** As papers come in, you are not trying to understand each one fully before moving to the next. You are pushing directly toward connection — "how does this relate to what I already have?" You use whatever non-linear format you can manage: arrows, maps, spatial groupings. The goal is to avoid stopping at understanding and to start building the relational network immediately.

**Stage 2: Cleaning and prioritizing.** At some point, the connections proliferate faster than you can organize them. This is a good sign — it means the higher-order thinking is activating. Now you evaluate: which connections are load-bearing? Which relationships are strong enough to anchor your argument? The network gets pruned, and the schema starts to solidify.

**Stage 3: Linearizing for output.** Only once the schema is solid do you convert it into prose. Linearizing is a different skill from schema-building — it is organizing spatial and relational thinking into sequential argument. Writing before the schema exists is just rearranging confusion. Most research delays happen because writers try to skip to stage 3 before stage 2 is complete.

## The Overwhelm Signal

Research contexts produce a specific flavor of overwhelm: too many tabs, too many PDFs, too many positions that partially contradict each other, and no clear entry point. The standard response is to simplify — pick a view and defend it, or narrow the question enough that the complexity disappears.

Sung's reframe: the overwhelm is the accurate representation of the field. It is not a sign that you're approaching it wrong; it is the signal that you have enough contact with the actual complexity to feel it. The work is to organize it, not to escape it. Retreat into premature simplification produces work that looks clean from the outside and is shallow from the inside — the gap between the argument you can make and the argument the field actually requires.

## Evidence Hierarchy and Its Limits

The clinical hierarchy — case reports, observational studies, RCTs, systematic reviews, meta-analyses — is a useful guide to weight of evidence but not a final verdict. Systematic reviews aggregate what has been studied; they cannot cover what has been neglected. A low-citation paper from a recent methodological perspective may carry more relevance for a specific research question than a high-citation meta-analysis from a different angle.

The practical implication: don't let evidence hierarchy replace your own judgment about what is relevant to your specific question. The hierarchy tells you about confidence in findings, not about topical fit. Use it as a filter, not a replacement for the higher-order evaluation of "does this actually speak to what I'm investigating?"

## AI in Research

The same LC60 rules apply here without modification. AI is valid for two purposes in a research context:

**Gap and perspective checking.** After developing a schema, you can ask AI to act as an expert and identify gaps or missed perspectives. You did the synthesis; AI flags what you might have missed. The value is in the schema you built, not in the AI's list.

**Brainstorming open questions.** Once you have a working model, AI can suggest angles, counterarguments, or adjacent considerations worth exploring. This is generative input into your further thinking, not a substitute for it.

What remains invalid in research as in learning: asking AI to evaluate which papers are most important, to summarize what you should think about a topic, or to suggest how your argument should be structured. Those are exactly the higher-order moves that determine whether research has value, and outsourcing them produces work that is indistinguishable from the surface but hollow underneath.

## Choosing Where to Start

For unfamiliar fields, Sung recommends beginning with the most-cited work in the area to build the rough big-picture scaffold, then tracing reference chains both forward and backward in time to build density. Research Rabbit is recommended over AI for source discovery — it surfaces relevant, specific, sometimes low-citation work that generalist AI models won't return because they over-index on popularity.
