# What Unifies the Enemies of Civilization?

## Core Thesis

**The enemies of civilisation are not unified by ideology — they are unified by epistemology. They all slow the growth of knowledge.**

Arjun Khemani and Logan Chipkin's essay identifies six distinct anti-civilisational memes — socialism, extreme environmentalism, scientism, relativism, dogmatism, and doomerism — and argues that beneath their surface differences lies a single structural failure. Each one, in its own way, suppresses the mechanism by which human civilisation advances: the conjecture and criticism of ideas. All progress — material, moral, scientific — depends on the ability to generate new ideas and subject them to error correction. Every enemy on the list either centralises control over that process, delegitimises the criticism of existing ideas, or convinces people that improvement is impossible.

The essay draws heavily on the framework of Karl Popper and F. A. Hayek, and opens with Marc Andreessen's *Techno-Optimist Manifesto* as an epigraph.

## The Six Enemies

### 1. Socialism

Socialism's structural failure is economic: central planning eliminates prices, and without prices, there is no mechanism for calculating how resources should be allocated. This is Mises's socialist calculation argument — not a political objection but an epistemological one. The central planner cannot know what the dispersed, real-time decisions of millions of economic actors would have revealed. Progress requires decentralised experimentation; socialism replaces it with top-down control.

The authors also challenge the fixed-pie assumption that underlies redistributive intuitions. Wealth is not finite; it is created. Humanity began in universal poverty. The answer to poverty is more wealth creation, not redistribution of a static sum. Yet 41% of American adults held favourable views of socialism in a 2021 poll — a generation that has not seen what central planning produces.

### 2. Extreme Environmentalism / Degrowth

The degrowth movement wants to reduce the material and energy throughput of the global economy — fewer children, less energy, less carbon. Khemani and Chipkin's counter-argument is not that environmental concerns are invalid but that the proposed solution inverts the actual causal structure.

Growth is not an abstraction serving capitalist interests — it is the mechanism by which humans solve problems. Climate-related disaster deaths have fallen by 98% over the last century as CO2 and temperatures have risen, because wealth allowed the construction of flood defences, hospitals, heat-resistant buildings, early warning systems. The capacity to solve environmental problems — to deflect asteroids, restore species, protect the biosphere — requires precisely the civilisational growth degrowthers want to reverse. More people means more creativity and more potential to solve problems that no current generation could solve alone. Depopulation removes the future solvers.

The authors are particularly sharp on nuclear power: environmentalists have been effective at blocking what they argue might be the single most consequential missed opportunity in energy history.

### 3. Scientism

Scientism is the conflation of scientific knowledge with all knowledge — the claim that "following the science" can answer political, moral, economic, and personal questions. F. A. Hayek coined the term and identified the error: applying the methods of physics to fields where those methods do not apply produces not rigour but pseudo-rigour.

Science can tell you how COVID-19 spreads and what conditions reduce transmission. It cannot tell you whether the trade-offs of lockdowns are worth it, whether vaccine development should receive public funding, or whether a grandparent should risk infection to see grandchildren. These questions require political, economic, moral, and personal knowledge — none of which comes from a lab. Those who claim otherwise are not being more scientific; they are being less, by treating contested normative questions as if they have empirical answers.

The practical danger: if scientific authority is invoked to foreclose debate on questions science cannot actually resolve, it functions as a mechanism for suppressing the criticism of ideas — i.e., dogmatism under a scientific flag.

### 4. Relativism

Moral relativism presents itself as open-minded — "who's to say who is in the wrong?" — but is neither open-minded nor fair. It is not open to the possibility that one party is right and the other wrong. It is not open to the idea that one society is dynamic and another static, one culture life-affirming and another death-worshipping. And it does no favours to the people trapped in static, closed societies: by refusing to say their situation could be better, it keeps them there.

The essay's examples are pointed: equivocating on October 7th by "ending the cycle of violence," treating Russia and Ukraine as symmetrically culpable, comparing Churchill to Hitler. In each case, the relativist refuses the cognitive work of distinguishing between the parties — which is precisely the work required to act correctly.

The practical damage to dynamic societies: relativism muddies their understanding of why they are successful, which undermines their ability to extend or defend the conditions that produced that success.

### 5. Dogmatism

Dogmatism is any idea treated as beyond criticism — a final truth, held with certainty, never to be revised. The authors note this is not exclusively religious. Political ideologies, scientific theories, and cultural frameworks can all be propagated dogmatically. The example given is Freudian psychoanalysis, which Popper identified as unfalsifiable — a framework that could absorb any counterevidence by reinterpreting it rather than revising the theory.

The mechanism of harm is direct: all ideas contain errors. Progress is made by identifying and correcting errors. Dogmatism shields ideas from the correction process. Even a small error, left uncorrected indefinitely, can compound.

### 6. Doomerism

Doomerism is the belief that human progress has hit a terminal limit — that the next innovation will be our undoing, that extinction is imminent, that we are uniquely vulnerable. The primary contemporary instantiation is AI doom: the fear that AGI will destroy humanity.

The authors' counter is two-part. First, a non-creative AI is as obedient as a microwave; safety concerns can be addressed incrementally, as with self-driving cars. Second, a genuinely AGI-level entity would face the same developmental challenge as a child: it would be shaped by the culture around it. The child analogy is instructive — humanity has been improving at raising children into productive adults for millennia, and the problem is not categorically different.

The practical danger of doomerism beyond the psychological: it justifies tyranny. If the next step is catastrophic, then restricting innovation is justified. But slowing innovation does not make the technology disappear — it ensures that whoever continues (including bad actors) gets there first.

## The Unifying Structure

Each of the six enemies maps onto the same underlying failure mode:

| Enemy | How it slows knowledge growth |
|-------|------------------------------|
| Socialism | Centralises resource allocation; eliminates price signals and entrepreneurial experimentation |
| Degrowth | Reduces the population and energy surplus required for civilisation-scale problem solving |
| Scientism | Forecloses moral/political debate by delegitimising non-scientific forms of knowledge |
| Relativism | Refuses to distinguish better from worse, preventing the spread of better ideas |
| Dogmatism | Immunises ideas against correction by treating them as beyond criticism |
| Doomerism | Neutralises effort by convincing people improvement is futile; justifies restricting innovation |

The essay's framework is explicitly Popperian: knowledge grows through conjecture and criticism. Any idea, institution, or cultural norm that restricts either conjecture (new ideas) or criticism (error correction) is an enemy of progress.

---

## What the Essay Doesn't Say

The essay is a manifesto more than a scholarly argument. It draws on Mises, Hayek, Popper, and Epstein but does not engage seriously with the strongest versions of the positions it critiques. The degrowth section, for instance, does not engage with ecological limits arguments beyond the one statistic on disaster deaths. The relativism section conflates epistemic relativism with moral equivocation. The socialism section addresses central planning but does not engage with social-democratic mixed-economy models.

This matters less as a criticism of the piece's conclusions than as a guide to where its argument is doing real work versus where it's moving quickly. The epistemological core — that all six enemies suppress the growth of knowledge — is the strongest part, and holds independently of the weaker applications.

## Open Questions

- Is there a version of the precautionary principle that is not epistemically catastrophic? Are there cases where slowing knowledge growth is justified — arms control, gain-of-function research? The essay's dismissal of the Precautionary Principle as "deeply immoral" is asserted rather than argued.
- Does the framework apply symmetrically? The essay focuses on left-coded enemies. Are there right-coded enemies of knowledge growth — nationalist protectionism, religious dogmatism, certain forms of conservatism — that fit the same analysis?
- What's the relationship between this framework and David Deutsch's *The Beginning of Infinity*, which explicitly argues that all problems are solvable given sufficient knowledge and that the only sin is closing off the search?
