# GPT - Live Clinic 64: Using iCS Techniques for Research

## Core Thesis

Research quality begins with organized expertise inside the researcher before it becomes writing, methodology, citations, or argument. The work is to hold a messy field of sources long enough to see relationships, gaps, assumptions, disagreements, missing perspectives, and the shape of the frontier. Notes and papers are downstream artifacts. The source of quality is the mental schema that can explain why each source matters, where it fits, what it fails to see, and what new knowledge could be created from the gap.

## Key Takeaways

- Research starts by making the field intelligible inside your own head.
- Every source has to be placed: what it supports, what it challenges, what it omits, and where it sits in the wider picture.
- Overwhelm is often the raw material of expertise before the field has been organized.
- Writing problems usually point upstream to schema problems, detail problems, or procedural writing problems.
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses are orientation tools, not final reality; they still inherit blind spots and measurement limits.
- AI is best used to widen the searchlight: missing perspectives, keywords, contrary views, and blind-spot checks after your own model exists.
- The researcher should be able to explain the field simply before narrowing into a specific question, method, or paper.

## Research Raises The Thinking Floor

Ordinary academic learning often lets the learner survive by understanding, remembering, and occasionally applying. Research does not. The task moves upward:

```text
understand source
-> locate source in wider field
-> compare against other views
-> detect gaps
-> evaluate strength and limitation
-> create a useful contribution
```

Each article is a puzzle piece. The useful move is to place it: where it belongs, what it touches, what it leaves empty, and whether the picture it suggests is distorted.

Research becomes inefficient when the learner keeps reading as if more exposure will eventually produce clarity. In practice, more reading often increases confusion until the field is actively organized.

## The Overwhelm Is The Work

The feeling of being buried under articles often marks first contact with the field before expertise has organized it.

```text
many papers
-> many perspectives
-> contradictory claims
-> unclear gaps
-> cognitive overload
-> temptation to summarize and file
```

The repair:

```text
stay with the overload
-> build provisional groups
-> compare claims
-> name disagreements
-> identify missing perspectives
-> revise the structure
-> explain the field simply
```

Expertise appears when the same mass of information starts feeling navigable. Dates, authors, findings, and citations become easier to remember because they have places to sit.

## Output Reveals The Source

Weak writing usually reflects weak internal organization. If the literature review has no narrative, if the discussion is hard to write, if the presentation cannot be compressed, the problem is usually upstream.

The diagnostic:

- If ideas are disordered, the higher-order schema is weak.
- If examples are missing, the lower-order detail is thin.
- If expression is clumsy despite clear thinking, the procedural writing skill needs practice.
- If methodology keeps changing, the research question may have narrowed too early.
- If every new paper creates confusion, the current map cannot absorb new evidence.

The repair often comes from reorganizing what has already been read rather than adding more sources to the pile.

## Learning Before Narrowing

Early narrowing makes research feel efficient but often creates drag. A narrow question before a broad schema forces every source through a small frame. The learner then fights the field instead of understanding it.

A stronger sequence:

```text
broad orientation
-> systematic reviews and major perspectives
-> contrary views
-> prominent authors
-> recurring methods and gaps
-> provisional map
-> focused question
-> study design or argument
```

The initial goal is field intelligibility. Once the field is intelligible, the writing can become narrow without becoming brittle.

## Evidence Is Still Perspective

The hierarchy of evidence is useful, but it can create false reverence. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses are powerful because they aggregate a field. They are limited because they still inherit the field's blind spots, the reviewers' framing, the available measures, and what prior researchers thought to study.

For emerging or difficult domains, the best available evidence may still miss the real problem. Learning science is a useful example because many older studies measured simplified memory tasks and then generalized far beyond the realistic learning environment.

The practical stance:

- Start with high-level reviews for orientation.
- Treat consensus as current best map, not final territory.
- Look for what was not measured.
- Look for time cost, ecological validity, population differences, and transfer.
- Ask which assumptions made the research easier to run but less useful.

Research begins when "what the literature says" becomes "what this literature can and cannot see."

## AI Belongs At The Edge, Not The Center

AI is useful for orientation, keyword discovery, finding possible missing perspectives, rewording, and scanning a brain dump for gaps. It is dangerous when used to create the literature review's real synthesis.

Research often lives at the edge of what is known. AI tends to pull from accessible, mainstream, and already-digested material. Paywalls, citation bias, shallow summaries, hallucinated nuance, and popular framing make it a poor substitute for building expertise.

Better uses:

- "Here is my current map. What perspectives might be missing?"
- "What keywords should I search to investigate this gap?"
- "What contrary positions exist around this claim?"
- "Find possible weaknesses in this explanation."
- "What prominent authors or concepts should I look for?"

Poor uses:

- "Summarize this field for me."
- "Write my literature review."
- "Tell me what the consensus is."
- "Explain the best theory."

The researcher should still read, compare, and decide. AI can widen the searchlight. It should not carry the map.

## How To Start A Research Topic

1. Find big-picture systematic reviews.
2. Build a rough field map.
3. Search for contrary or minority perspectives.
4. Identify the handful of authors who repeatedly move the field.
5. Follow their publication histories.
6. Use citation networks and tools like Research Rabbit to find adjacent work.
7. Email researchers when access blocks the source.
8. Keep revising the map until the field starts feeling coherent.

The high-value move is finding the small group of researchers actually pushing the boundary. Many papers add minor extensions. A few authors define the shape of the problem.

## What It Should Feel Like

Good research processing feels like increasing command over a messy territory. The work is still hard, but the confusion changes character.

Good signs:

- papers start falling into recognizable camps;
- author names and dates become memorable without brute force;
- gaps become more specific;
- writing starts having a natural order;
- questions become sharper;
- the field feels arguable, not just readable.

Warning signs:

- every paper becomes a standalone summary;
- notes grow while the map stays vague;
- AI summaries feel clearer than your own understanding;
- the research question narrows before the field is understood;
- more reading produces more confusion without new structure;
- writing begins before the mental schema can explain itself.

## Questions Worth Asking

- What is the field currently failing to measure?
- Which sources are central, and which are derivative?
- Which author or school of thought is quietly shaping the entire conversation?
- What would I need to explain this field in eight minutes?
- What part of my literature map is still just a pile?
- Where am I using AI because I do not want to do the hard synthesis?
