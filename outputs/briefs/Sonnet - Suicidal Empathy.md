# Suicidal Empathy: Dying to Be Kind

## Core Thesis

**Empathy, when unregulated by reason, becomes self-destructive — and Western elites have miscalibrated it to the point of civilizational danger.**

Gad Saad's central argument is that empathy is not inherently good or bad, but like any adaptive trait, it can misfire. When people prioritise the feelings of ostensibly marginalised groups over rational assessment of consequences, they engage in what Saad calls "suicidal empathy": an orgiastic, hyperactive form of empathy deployed on the wrong targets. The individual gains social status from performing compassion. The collective pays the cost — in crime, in eroded norms, in policy failures that protect perpetrators over victims.

This is his sixth book and a direct continuation of *The Parasitic Mind* (2021), which argued that progressive ideas spread like cognitive viruses. *Suicidal Empathy* narrows the diagnosis: the vector is not bad ideas in general, but specifically the hijacking of the empathy module. Published May 12 2026, it debuted at Amazon #16 in books.

---

## The Concept: What Is Suicidal Empathy?

Saad defines suicidal empathy as the inability to implement optimal decisions when psychologically conditioned to prioritise empathy — or the performance of empathy — over a rational course of action.

The key distinction he draws is between *calibrated empathy* (understanding others in a way informed by reality) and *misfired empathy* (projecting one's own psychology onto others without accounting for genuine differences in values, personality, or intent). The second version is the problem.

**The structural error:** When someone asks "what would it take *for me* to commit this crime?", they anchor their empathy in their own psychology. If they themselves would only commit violence under conditions of extreme trauma and deprivation, they assume the same is true of the perpetrator. This produces bad policy — treating criminal behaviour as a symptom requiring therapeutic intervention when it may simply reflect a different disposition.

**The social dynamic:** Saad argues that misfired empathy also has a performative function. Expressing maximal compassion for stigmatised groups confers status within elite progressive circles. This creates an incentive structure where correct empathy-signalling matters more than accurate assessment of consequences. The individual benefits socially; the group suffers the downstream effects.

**The evolutionary framing:** Saad draws on evolutionary psychology to argue that empathy evolved as an adaptive mechanism — it facilitated cooperation, mate selection, and group cohesion. Like all evolved traits, it can misfire in environments different from those it evolved in. His analogy: female sperm whales have a "Marguerite formation" behaviour — circling to protect calves — that evolved for the wild but makes them easier to kill by hunters. A trait that was adaptive becomes maladaptive in a changed context.

## Key Examples and Applications

Saad applies the concept across several domains:

**Criminal justice.** Progressive approaches to crime that focus exclusively on trauma and systemic factors, he argues, fail to account for individual differences in conscientiousness and propensity for violence. The result: policies that protect perpetrators while leaving victims unaddressed. He uses a vivid real-world case: a young woman who declined to prosecute a subway attacker out of reluctance to "send another black man to jail," only for him to later allegedly kill an elderly man.

**Immigration.** Prioritising empathy for illegal immigrants over the security interests of citizens, in Saad's framing, is a case where the compassion extended to one group structurally disadvantages another — veterans, disaster victims, working-class communities absorbing economic strain.

**Trans sport.** Saad argues that empathy for trans women in athletic competition has been extended at the direct expense of the rights of cis women — a zero-sum application where empathy for one group requires the other to absorb a concrete loss.

**Terrorism and jihadism.** Left-wing commentators, he argues, explain terrorism solely through Western foreign policy because they have no framework for understanding that some ideologies have internal motivations not reducible to Western provocation. Projecting one's own aversion to violence onto groups that do not share it produces systematic misdiagnosis.

**Language policing.** Saad is particularly sharp on renaming: "undocumented" for illegal, "unhoused" for homeless. His satirical extension — proposing "undocumented lovemaker" and "altruistic sperm donor" as terms for rapists — is designed to expose the absurdity of softening language for inimical actors as a form of misfired compassion.

## The "Suicide" Framework

A distinct section of the book grounds the title. Saad surveys historical and cross-cultural instances of individual and group suicide — human sacrifice, honour suicides, *shinju* (mutual suicide by lovers in Japanese tradition), Heaven's Gate, Jonestown, the kamikaze — and argues these had cultural functions. The framework: some forms of self-destruction have been adaptive at the group level (reinforcing social norms, demonstrating commitment, removing shame).

He then pivots to argue that the West's current form of collective self-sacrifice — extending rights and resources to groups actively hostile to Western values — is a unique and non-adaptive form of civilizational suicide. This is where critics find the argument most underdeveloped (see below).

## Critical Reception

The book landed to a genuinely mixed reception, including from within broadly sympathetic outlets.

**What critics concede:** Across hostile and friendly reviews, there is consistent acknowledgement that the core intuition is not wrong. Empathy *can* misfire. Projecting one's own psychology onto people who do not share it *does* produce bad analysis and bad policy. The performative dimension of compassion-signalling in elite circles is a real phenomenon. These points are granted even by critics who find the book otherwise inadequate.

**The Quillette critique (Ben Sixsmith):** From a broadly sympathetic outlet, Sixsmith's verdict is damaging: the book is "almost impossible to read." Saad, he argues, "rambles smugly between subjects and tends to conflate sarcasm with wit." The self-referential structure — constant callbacks to his own books, podcasts, and Twitter feuds — makes sustained argument difficult to follow. The concept of "suicidal empathy" is a genuinely interesting neighbourhood; Saad takes readers on an exhaustive tour of an entire metropolis while bragging the whole time.

**The UnHerd critique (Valerie Stivers):** More pointed on the intellectual substance. Stivers argues the book's evolutionary framework is used loosely rather than rigorously. Saad declares early that academic distinctions in empathy research are "unnecessary for our current treatment" — a dismissal that undermines the scientific authority the book then claims. His examples (the Silence of the Lambs Buffalo Bill scene to illustrate empathy's "darker side"; a single sperm whale behaviour to prove evolutionary misfiring) are vivid but prove little. The suicide framework raises a question Saad does not address: if some suicides are evolutionarily adaptive, could there be something beneficial about the current "suicidal empathy"? The book doesn't engage this.

Stivers's deeper point: Saad has hold of a real moral conflict — the tension between self-defence and altruism, between protecting one's group and extending care to strangers — but tries to resolve it using evolutionary psychology, which "either doesn't have the tools, or doesn't seem to in his hands." The result is vitriol where rigour is needed.

**The Wikipedia / academic framing:** The concept has no support in academic psychology. Saad is a professor of marketing, not a psychologist or sociologist. Wikipedia notes the phrase is "commonly used by culture warriors, far-right figures, and Christian nationalists to condemn empathy towards groups such as immigrants and racial minorities." Historian of psychology Susan Lanzoni describes the disparagement of empathy as "a structured attempt to dehumanize people who might be in need of it."

**Cultural reach:** Despite mixed reviews, the concept has achieved significant circulation. Elon Musk popularised "civilizational suicidal empathy" on Joe Rogan's podcast in 2025. JD Vance's framing of restricted empathy for migrants drew a direct rebuke from Pope Francis. The term has been compared to the Great Replacement theory in its underlying logic — the claim that Western societies face destruction at the hands of groups that have been given too much compassion.

## Where the Argument Is Strongest

The book is most persuasive in the mid-range cases: where empathy has been extended in ways that produce concrete, demonstrable harm to third parties, and where the compassion in question is clearly performative rather than practically constructive.

The subway case is genuinely troubling and not easily dismissed. Progressives who explain away crime through trauma exclusively — and who resist policing and prosecution as inherently oppressive — do sometimes produce policies under which victims are sacrificed for ideological consistency. Saad's point that projecting one's own psychology onto perpetrators leads to systematic misdiagnosis has real force.

The performative dimension is also well-observed. Competitive compassion-signalling is a documented social phenomenon in elite institutional contexts. Saad is not inventing this.

---

## Where the Argument Breaks Down

**The evolutionary framing is decorative, not structural.** Citing evolutionary psychology in the introduction and then abandoning it for political commentary doesn't make the political commentary scientific. The sperm whale analogy, the survey of historical suicides — these establish atmosphere, not evidence.

**The concept is applied asymmetrically.** Saad critiques empathy extended to criminals, immigrants, trans people, and jihadists, but does not apply the same framework to empathy misfiring in other directions — e.g., excessive empathy for in-group members who commit harm, or the way nationalism can itself be a form of misfired tribal loyalty. The selectivity undermines the universalist framing.

**The suicide framework is incomplete.** If some self-destructive behaviours are adaptive at the group level, the book needed to explain precisely why Western empathy toward outgroups fails that test — not assert it. The assertion is doing the work that the argument should be doing.

**The altruism counter-case goes unaddressed.** The book of Exodus instructs care for the stranger because the Israelites were once strangers in Egypt. Altruism and self-sacrifice are virtues with multi-thousand-year traditions across cultures. That empathy toward strangers has sometimes been adaptive — and has built the cosmopolitan institutions the West claims to value — is not engaged.

---

## Contextual Note

*Suicidal Empathy* is best read as a polemical contribution to an ongoing cultural debate rather than as a work of psychology or evolutionary science. The concept is useful as a conversational frame — a way of naming the phenomenon of compassion that causes concrete harm to third parties through misaligned priorities. As a rigorous theory of human behaviour, it does not hold up to scrutiny. As a critique of specific progressive policy failures, some of its examples land. The delivery is the obstacle: the book's rhetorical style undercuts its credibility with the audiences most likely to be persuaded by a well-made version of the argument.

## Open Questions

- Is there a version of this critique — more precisely scoped, more empirically grounded — that survives the methodological objections? Who makes it?
- What does the actual empathy research literature say about calibrated vs. uncalibrated empathy? Paul Bloom's *Against Empathy* covers adjacent ground with more academic rigour.
- Is the performative compassion phenomenon (status-seeking through empathy signalling) documented in the social psychology literature, and under what conditions does it produce worse policy outcomes?
- What is the correct response to the altruism counter-case? When is empathy toward strangers adaptive and when is it genuinely self-destructive?
