# GPT - MG and Kolbs Template

## Core Thesis

The MG and Kolbs template is a continuous improvement system built around attempts, evaluations, and follow-on experiments. The full Notion version has three connected zones: Action turns priorities into scheduled work, Progress turns goals into processes and marginal gains, and Learning turns study sessions into spaced retrieval, generated questions, and exam feedback. The useful core is the Progress loop: choose a goal, dissect the process, run a real attempt, reflect through Kolbs, extract a marginal gain, and schedule the next experiment.

## Key Takeaways

- The template is larger than MG and Kolbs. It is a command center with Action, Progress, Learning, and Second Brain layers.
- MG and Kolbs sit mainly in the Progress zone, but they depend on the Action zone to schedule attempts and the Learning zone when the goal is academic.
- Goals are translated into processes. Processes become skills, marginal metrics, Kolbs cycles, and experiments.
- Kolbs cycles work best as chains. A completed Kolb creates or informs the next Kolb, preserving continuity across attempts.
- Marginal Gains supply the improvement target. Kolbs supplies the reflection loop. Tasks supply the calendar entry where the next attempt actually happens.
- Weekly evaluation is the system reset. It compares the current process against the target level, chooses the next focus, and creates marginal metrics for the week.
- The template is powerful because it links goals, skills, tasks, Kolbs, experiments, and review. It is risky because the machinery can become heavier than the improvement it supports.
- The Obsidian version should preserve the loop and reduce the database overhead.

## The Operating Model

```text
goal
-> dissect into controllable processes
-> identify current skill level and target level
-> choose active process
-> schedule a task or attempt
-> perform the attempt
-> run Kolbs reflection
-> extract marginal gains and experiments
-> schedule the next attempt
-> evaluate weekly
-> update the goal, skill, or process
```

The template works when every object points back to action. A goal points to a process. A process points to an attempt. An attempt creates evidence. Evidence produces a marginal gain. The marginal gain becomes the next experiment.

## The Three Zones

### Action

Action is the execution layer.

It handles:

- task capture;
- prioritization through an Eisenhower-style matrix;
- daily scheduling;
- mini-tasks;
- pockets of time;
- focus training;
- office breaks;
- experiments as actionable tasks.

The key idea is that tasks should become small enough to schedule. Large intentions are broken into mini-tasks. The top priorities get protected calendar time first. Lower-priority or flexible tasks move into pockets.

The Action zone is most useful when the system needs real attempts. Without a scheduled attempt, Goals and Kolbs can become reflection without data.

### Progress

Progress is the improvement layer.

It handles:

- goals;
- anchored goals;
- dissected processes;
- current and target levels;
- competency stage;
- performance goals;
- weekly evaluations;
- marginal metrics;
- Kolbs cycles;
- follow-on experiments.

This is the heart of the MG and Kolbs system. A goal is too large to improve directly. It has to be broken into skills, processes, and behaviors that can be observed and changed.

The Progress zone should answer:

```text
What am I trying to improve?
What process controls that improvement?
What level am I at now?
What would 10/10 look like?
What is the next marginal gain?
What experiment will test it?
```

### Learning

Learning is the academic study layer.

It handles:

- SIR sessions;
- subjects and modules;
- syllabus pages;
- generated questions;
- question papers;
- gap lists;
- problem solving worksheets;
- pre-exam review.

The Learning zone extends the same improvement logic into study. A retrieval session finds gaps. Gaps become questions. Questions connect back to topics and subjects. Past papers expose patterns in mistakes. Those patterns become process improvements.

This matters because the learning system should not only store study records. It should make the next study session more precise.

### Second Brain

Second Brain is the storage layer.

It holds documents, subject pages, resources, life notes, and knowledge networks. It is intentionally flexible. The risk is that it can grow without feeding the Action, Progress, or Learning loops.

For this vault, the public knowledge base already acts as a more advanced second brain. The MG and Kolbs area should avoid duplicating that role. Its job is improvement, not storage.

## Goals Into Processes

A goal becomes usable when it is dissected into controllable processes.

The template pushes this question:

```text
What do I need to be good at to achieve this goal?
```

The answer should focus on process, not outcome. Outcomes are symptoms of the process. Processes are the part that can be trained, tracked, reflected on, and improved.

Example:

```text
goal: improve Vietnamese
-> process: daily comprehensible input
-> skill: noticing useful phrases
-> marginal gain: write one sentence after each clip
-> Kolb trigger: after three sessions, reflect on what blocked consistency
```

A good goal page should contain:

- the desired outcome;
- the processes that produce it;
- the skills needed;
- the current level;
- the target level;
- performance goals;
- weekly evaluation links;
- active Kolbs;
- next experiment.

## Kolbs As Chains

Kolbs should run in chains, not isolated reflections.

Each cycle should preserve:

- previous Kolb;
- current attempt;
- skill or process;
- marginal gain being tested;
- experiment;
- next Kolb.

This creates continuity:

```text
SIR 1
-> experiment
-> SIR 2
-> refined experiment
-> SIR 3
```

The important unit is the improving process, not the single reflection.

The template's button that completes the current Kolb and opens the next one reflects this idea. The mechanic is Notion-specific, but the principle transfers cleanly to Obsidian: each Kolb should end by naming the next experiment and linking the next cycle.

## Marginal Metrics

Marginal metrics make the next improvement visible during the week.

They come from:

- the current active process;
- the weekly evaluation;
- a Kolb insight;
- a process bottleneck;
- a small behavior worth repeating.

The metric should be small enough to check daily and meaningful enough to affect the process.

Examples:

- one SIR session scheduled immediately after finishing the current one;
- one Vietnamese clip summarized after immersion;
- one pre-work plan before starting an agentic engineering session;
- one focus block with phone out of reach;
- one Kolb after a failed study attempt;
- one sentence identifying the next bottleneck after each workout.

Marginal metrics should feel like handles, not dashboards.

## Weekly Evaluation

Weekly evaluation is the system's calibration point.

The useful sequence:

```text
recheck target level
-> rate current level
-> justify the rating with evidence
-> identify the active process
-> choose the next marginal metric
-> schedule attempts
```

The rating matters less than the justification. The point is to make the learner inspect why progress happened or failed.

Good evaluation questions:

- What improved this week?
- What stayed stuck?
- Which process explains the result?
- What would 10/10 look like?
- What is the current competency stage?
- What is the next marginal gain?
- What attempt will generate useful data next week?

The weekly evaluation protects the system from drifting into task completion without process improvement.

## Action Zone Adaptation For Obsidian

The Notion version relies on buttons, repeated pages, relation properties, status fields, and calendar integration. Obsidian can support the same logic, but the version should be lighter.

Useful Obsidian translation:

| Notion mechanic | Obsidian equivalent |
|---|---|
| Database relation | Wikilink |
| Status property | YAML field or plain table |
| Button creates next Kolb | Manual link to next Kolb |
| Calendar task | External calendar or dated note |
| Weekly recurring page | Journal calendar entry or repeating reminder |
| Linked database view | Index page with tables |
| Mini-task | Checklist item under active attempt |

The Obsidian version should avoid rebuilding Notion inside Markdown. The better move is to preserve the loop:

```text
goal -> skill -> attempt -> Kolb -> marginal gain -> next attempt
```

## What To Keep

Keep the parts that change behavior:

- Goals as direction.
- Skills as trainable capabilities.
- Processes as the controllable middle layer.
- Kolbs as reflection after attempts.
- Marginal Gains as the next small improvement.
- Weekly evaluation as calibration.
- SIR/gap tracking when the work is academic.
- Calendar scheduling for attempts that must actually happen.

These parts support recurrence, emotional engagement, identity reinforcement, visible progress, and action initiation.

## What To Compress

Compress the parts that create maintenance drag:

- large task databases;
- many placeholder pages;
- complicated relation fields;
- duplicate database views;
- Notion-specific buttons;
- automatic repeated pages that are not actively used;
- Second Brain storage inside the MG/Kolbs area;
- detailed Learning Zone infrastructure unless the goal is currently academic.

The public website version should be smaller than the Notion template. The private Obsidian version can contain more working structure, but only if it is actively used.

## Suggested Public Page Shape

The public `MG & Kolbs` page should explain the operating loop and link to clean templates. It should not expose private goals, filled-in Kolbs, or fake example rows.

Recommended structure:

```markdown
# MG & Kolbs

## Operating Loop
Goal -> process -> attempt -> Kolb -> marginal gain -> next attempt.

## Active Improvement Areas
Priority 0 areas and public-safe notes.

## Templates
- Goals
- Skills
- Kolbs
- Weekly Evaluation

## How To Use
1. Pick one active area.
2. Name the process bottleneck.
3. Choose one marginal gain.
4. Schedule one attempt.
5. Reflect with Kolbs.
6. Carry the next experiment forward.

## Related Wiki Pages
- Marginal Gains
- Kolbs Experiential Cycle
- Priority 0
- Balancing Multiple Interests
```

## Suggested Private Page Shape

The private version can be more operational:

```text
Priority 0
-> Goals Index
-> Skills Index
-> Active Goal
-> Active Skill
-> Current Kolb
-> Next Attempt
-> Weekly Evaluation
```

Keep four working views:

1. **Today / Next Attempt**: what gets done next.
2. **Active Goals**: what is currently alive.
3. **Open Kolbs**: which reflections need completion.
4. **Weekly Evaluation**: what gets reviewed on schedule.

The private version should be allowed to contain friction, mess, and personal data. The public version should show the pattern.

## Failure Modes

| Failure | What It Looks Like | Repair |
|---|---|---|
| Dashboard drag | The system becomes a place to maintain pages instead of improve behavior. | Cut views until the next attempt is obvious. |
| Fake reflection | Kolbs are written without a real attempt. | Require evidence: friction, result, surprise, failure, or feedback. |
| Goal vagueness | Goals stay as outcomes without processes. | Dissect into controllable skills and behaviors. |
| Metric theater | Marginal metrics are tracked because they are easy, not useful. | Tie each metric to a process bottleneck. |
| Notion mimicry | Obsidian tries to replicate database automation. | Use links, templates, and simple indexes. |
| Learning sprawl | SIR, question banks, and papers expand beyond current needs. | Activate Learning Zone pieces only for active academic goals. |
| Reflection loops | Kolbs generate insight but no next attempt. | End every Kolb with an experiment and date. |
| Over-integration | Every goal, task, skill, and note must relate to everything. | Link only the relationships that help the next action. |

## What It Should Feel Like

The system should feel like returning to a bench where the next tool is already laid out.

Good signs:

- one active process is obvious;
- the next attempt has a time or trigger;
- the current Kolb points to a concrete experiment;
- weekly evaluation produces one or two marginal metrics;
- goals feel less abstract because they have skills attached;
- skills feel trainable because they have attempts attached;
- reflection produces a change in behavior.

Warning signs:

- the page invites browsing more than action;
- the user spends more time maintaining properties than running attempts;
- every table has stale examples;
- Kolbs become emotional journaling without experiments;
- goals look inspiring but do not create a next session;
- the system creates guilt because too many areas appear active at once.

[[private/Goals/Priority 0|Priority 0]] should act as the identity layer. It can hold every active or parked area without demanding that all of them receive weekly action.

[[wiki/Syntheses/Balancing Multiple Interests - Breadth v Focus|Balancing Multiple Interests: Breadth v Focus]] should govern activation. Breadth belongs in Priority 0. Focus belongs in the active loop.

[[wiki/Workflows/Knowledge Base as Thinking Partner|Knowledge Base as Thinking Partner]] should connect to MG and Kolbs by turning synthesis into attempts. A note becomes useful when it changes the next experiment.

The current `mg-kolbs` page should probably become a public-facing operating room: lean, direct, and action-biased. The deeper private system can live locally in Obsidian.
