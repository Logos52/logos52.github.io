# What Unifies the Enemies of Civilization?

## Core Thesis

All durable enemies of civilization share one operating property: **they slow the growth of knowledge**.

Socialism, extreme environmentalism/degrowth, scientism, moral relativism, dogmatism, and doomerism are not random bad ideas. They are different surface expressions of the same underlying function — reducing the rate at which useful, testable knowledge is discovered, criticized, and applied at scale.

Civilization advances when knowledge grows. It stagnates or regresses when knowledge growth is systematically impeded. This is the single variable that unifies the enemies.

## Operating Model

```text
Idea or policy enters the system
-> claims moral or epistemic superiority
-> restricts experimentation, criticism, or resource allocation
-> reduces variance in approaches and speed of feedback
-> slows the rate of useful knowledge generation
-> compounds over time into civilizational drag
```

The repair loop is the inverse:

```text
Proposed constraint on action or speech
-> ask: does this increase or decrease the rate of knowledge growth?
-> map who loses the ability to experiment and learn
-> reject if the primary effect is knowledge suppression
```

## The Six Enemies as Knowledge Brakes

**1. Socialism**  
Central planning removes price signals. Without prices, economic calculation becomes impossible. Entrepreneurs can no longer discover which resource combinations actually improve human lives. The result is not just inefficiency — it is the systematic destruction of the discovery process itself. Knowledge about what people value and how to produce it stops being generated.

**2. Extreme Environmentalism / Degrowth**  
Degrowth explicitly advocates reducing material and energy throughput. This is a direct attack on the scale at which experimentation can occur. Fewer people, less energy, and lower consumption all reduce the number of trials humanity can run. Since most progress comes from high-variance, high-scale experimentation, deliberate shrinkage of the experimental base is a knowledge-growth suppressor.

**3. Scientism**  
The belief that scientific knowledge is the only valid form of knowledge. This disables the other necessary engines of knowledge growth: moral reasoning, economic calculation, political judgment, and philosophical criticism. When complex trade-offs (lockdowns vs. education, energy abundance vs. emissions, inclusion vs. fairness) are declared "follow the science" problems, the non-scientific forms of knowledge required to make the actual decision are suppressed.

**4. Moral Relativism**  
The refusal to judge one society, culture, or set of values as better or worse than another. This removes the selective pressure that would otherwise allow better ideas to spread and worse ones to be discarded. If all systems are equally valid, there is no drive to copy the practices that demonstrably produce more knowledge, wealth, and freedom. Relativism protects static societies from the clarifying effect of comparison.

**5. Dogmatism**  
Any idea treated as uncriticizable. Dogmatism locks knowledge into a frozen state. Because all ideas contain errors, the refusal to allow criticism guarantees that those errors persist and compound. The more important the domain (AI alignment, governance, education, medicine), the more dangerous the dogmatic lock-in becomes.

**6. Doomerism**  
The belief that further progress is either impossible or actively dangerous. Doomerism justifies preemptive constraint: if the next step risks catastrophe, then slowing down, regulating heavily, or banning becomes the responsible position. This creates a self-reinforcing loop where fear of the unknown reduces the very experimentation required to make the unknown safe.

## The Unifying Mechanism

Every one of these positions ultimately reduces **variance + feedback speed**.

- Socialism centralizes and removes price discovery.
- Degrowth shrinks the number of people and resources available for trials.
- Scientism narrows the types of questions that can be asked.
- Relativism removes the incentive to select superior models.
- Dogmatism prevents error correction.
- Doomerism preemptively limits the exploration space.

The common output is the same: fewer new, useful distinctions are made per unit of time and resources.

## Why This Unification Matters

Once you see the pattern, you stop arguing about surface morality ("is degrowth compassionate?") and start asking the diagnostic question:

> "Does this idea or policy increase or decrease the rate at which humanity generates useful, testable knowledge?"

This single question cuts through most civilizational debates more cleanly than traditional left/right or progressive/conservative framing.

Ideas that survive this filter tend to be pro-experimentation, pro-criticism, pro-scale, and pro-selection. Ideas that fail it, regardless of their emotional packaging, function as brakes on the only process that has ever lifted humanity out of poverty and weakness: the compounding growth of knowledge.
