# GPT - How to Get Rich

## Core Thesis

Naval's wealth framework argues that getting rich without getting lucky comes from combining specific knowledge, accountability, leverage, judgment, and long-term games. Wealth is not money or status. Wealth is ownership of assets that earn independently of direct labor.

The source is a systematic philosophy of escaping rented time. The goal is to become someone who can create, own, and scale valuable judgment.

## Compressed Takeaways

1. **Seek wealth, not money or status.** Money transfers wealth; status is social rank; wealth is productive ownership.
2. **Do not rent out all your time.** Time-for-money labor cannot scale enough.
3. **Give society what it wants but does not know how to get.** Scarcity plus demand creates opportunity.
4. **Specific knowledge is the foundation.** It is hard to train, hard to outsource, and often tied to curiosity.
5. **Learn to sell and learn to build.** Either skill creates leverage; together they compound.
6. **Use permissionless leverage.** Code and media scale without requiring someone else's approval.
7. **Take accountability.** Visible responsibility creates trust, upside, and equity.
8. **Play long-term games with long-term people.** Reputation and relationships compound.
9. **Judgment is the decisive skill.** Leverage magnifies both good and bad decisions.
10. **Productize yourself.** Combine authenticity, specific knowledge, leverage, and accountability.

## Wealth vs Money vs Status

The opening distinction is foundational:

- **Wealth**: assets that earn while you sleep.
- **Money**: a social technology for transferring time and wealth.
- **Status**: position in a hierarchy.

Many people chase money or status while missing wealth. Status games are zero-sum. Wealth creation can be positive-sum because it creates abundance.

This matters for the knowledge base because a public body of work can become a form of permissionless leverage. Writing, code, notes, tools, and media can compound without requiring direct labor each time.

## Specific Knowledge

Specific knowledge is knowledge that cannot easily be trained into someone else. It often emerges from curiosity, obsession, experience, technical skill, creativity, taste, or unusual combinations.

It is usually:

- discovered through play,
- built through apprenticeship or self-directed learning,
- hard to automate directly,
- tied to personal judgment,
- expressed through products, media, code, or business.

This connects to [[wiki/Concepts/Knowledge Mastery - From Recognition to Usable Knowledge|Knowledge Mastery: From Recognition to Usable Knowledge]]. The goal is not generic knowledge. The goal is knowledge that becomes judgment and action.

## Leverage

Naval distinguishes older and newer forms of leverage:

- labor,
- capital,
- code,
- media,
- products.

Labor and capital often require permission. Code and media are more permissionless. AI extends this pattern by making code, media, research, and automation cheaper to produce.

This links to [[wiki/Concepts/Agentic Engineering|Agentic Engineering]]. AI agents are a new form of leverage, but only if the human has enough judgment to direct them.

## Accountability

Accountability means putting your name on the line. It creates downside, but it also creates upside. In a leveraged world, anonymous or interchangeable work captures less value.

Practical forms of accountability:

- publish under your name,
- own the decision,
- accept criticism,
- take responsibility for outcomes,
- build reputation over time.

This is relevant to the user's public website. Publishing a knowledge base is not only archiving notes; it is a small act of accountability.

## Judgment

The source repeatedly returns to judgment. Leverage without judgment is dangerous. The more leverage a person controls, the more important each decision becomes.

Judgment includes:

- choosing the right thing to work on,
- picking collaborators,
- avoiding ruin,
- recognizing incentives,
- knowing when advice does not apply,
- avoiding status traps,
- understanding compounding.

This connects to [[wiki/Decision Making/Decision Making|Decision Making]], [[wiki/Decision Making/Good Decisions|Good Decisions]], and [[wiki/Red Team/Applied Critical Thinking - Testing Frames|Applied Critical Thinking: Testing Frames]].

## Productize Yourself

The framework culminates in productizing yourself:

1. Identify specific knowledge.
2. Apply leverage.
3. Take accountability.
4. Use authenticity to escape competition.
5. Build long-term assets.

Authenticity matters because competition is most intense when everyone is trying to be the same thing. Specific knowledge is often personal. The more the work fits the person, the harder it is to copy.

## Mental Models

The later sections introduce mental models that support wealth creation:

- principal-agent problems,
- Kelly criterion,
- Schelling points,
- long-term games,
- compounding relationships,
- price discrimination,
- consumer surplus,
- net present value,
- externalities.

These are not abstract business-school terms. They are tools for seeing incentives, risk, cooperation, and value more clearly.

## Relevance to This Knowledge Base

This source supports several possible directions:

- Treat the public knowledge base as permissionless media leverage.
- Treat agentic engineering as code leverage.
- Treat learning systems as a way to build specific knowledge.
- Treat writing and notes as reputation-building artifacts.
- Use judgment to decide what to publish, build, or ignore.

The connection is not "get rich by taking notes." It is that durable public artifacts can compound when they reflect specific knowledge and real judgment.
