# Opus - How to Remember Everything You Read

## Core Thesis

Sung argues that "remembering everything you read" is the wrong goal — Kim Peek had perfect recall and couldn't reason through a college exam. The right goal is *remembering everything you need*, in a form you can use. Two stages must stay balanced: consumption and digestion. Most people overdose on consumption (read more, listen at 2x, binge tutorials) and underdo digestion. The fix is the **PACER** classification system — Procedural, Analogous, Conceptual, Evidence, Reference — each with its own digestion process. Match the process to the information type or waste your time.

## Compressed Takeaways

1. **Consumption and digestion must stay balanced.** Overconsumption without digestion produces forgetting (up to 90% of what's read). Slow consumption to keep digestion possible.
2. **Goal isn't remembering everything; it's remembering what you'll use, in a usable form.** Perfect recall (Kim Peek) loses to high-order reasoning every time at adult-stakes exams.
3. **PACER classifies information; each type has a targeted digestion process.**
4. **Procedural → practice early.** Don't memorize procedures; apply them as soon as possible.
5. **Analogous → critique the analogy.** Find what's similar, what's different, where it breaks. The critique is the deep processing.
6. **Conceptual → map nonlinearly.** Mind-map / chunk-map. Concepts live in networks; replicate the network, don't memorize the linearization.
7. **Evidence → store and rehearse.** Note it down (notion / second brain / flashcards). Rehearse later via *application* — using the evidence to support concepts.
8. **Reference → store and rehearse via spaced repetition.** Pure recall info — flash cards (Anki) is right. Don't waste time on this during reading.
9. **Don't try to memorize during consumption.** Reading-and-rereading-to-memorize is the most common time waste. Each information type has a *later* process for retention.
10. **If you can't digest now, slow down.** Slow consumption beats overconsume-and-forget. The future-you doing re-reading is paying interest on the present's overconsumption.

## The PACER Decision Tree

The system runs in two stages and the classification happens at consumption time:

**Stage 1 — Consume.** As you read or listen, classify each piece of information:

| Type | What it looks like | Digestion process |
|---|---|---|
| **P**rocedural | How to do something — steps, techniques, workflows | Apply / practice early in real context |
| **A**nalogous | Reminds you of something you already know | Critique the analogy — similarities, differences, breakdown points |
| **C**onceptual | Facts, theories, principles, relationships | Nonlinear mapping — chunk map / mind map |
| **E**vidence | Specific examples, dates, statistics, cases that prove a concept | Store now, rehearse later via application |
| **R**eference | Pure detail (constants, names, lookup info) | Store now, rehearse via spaced repetition (Anki) |

**Stage 2 — Digest.** Apply the targeted process for each type. This is where retention is built. Without it, consumption goes to waste.

The key claim: there is no shortcut. Trying to memorize evidence or reference info *during* reading is what eats the time you needed for proper conceptual mapping. The order is wrong. Conceptual mapping during reading; evidence and reference rehearsal *after*.

## Procedural and the "I Don't Have Time to Practice" Problem

Sung's clinical example: medical school physical exam techniques are procedural. The right move is to learn the procedure and immediately practice on a friend, family member, or model. Wait two weeks to practice and most of it is gone.

What if you don't have practice time right now? Either move on to something else, or stop consuming and wait. Don't waste time trying to memorize procedure — it's the lowest-yield digestion possible for procedural info. The only memory worth building is the embodied one.

This connects to the broader balance principle: each consumption-without-digestion accumulates as future-debt. Pretend you're learning is the worst version of learning.

## Analogous and the Critique Move

Most people generate analogies and stop there. "Muscle contraction reminds me of swimming." Done.

Sung's add: that's where the digestion *starts*. Critique the analogy. Where exactly is it similar? Where does it break? What conditions make the analogy hold? When does it fail? Is there a better one?

The critique is the deep processing. Without it, the analogy is just a pleasant aside. With it, the analogy becomes a structural connection in your knowledge network — pinning the new information to existing prior knowledge with explicit constraints.

## Conceptual and Mapping

Conceptual information is the bulk of most learning, especially in any science or humanities. Sung's process is the chunk-map: nonlinear, networked, lateral connections, 2-4 items per group. The map grows as you read. Things move around, get reorganized, get re-clustered.

The mapping is doing two things simultaneously: it's the digestion process for the conceptual info, and it's also the scaffolding into which evidence and reference info can be slotted later. A well-built conceptual map is what makes evidence rehearsal efficient — you have somewhere for each piece of evidence to attach.

If you can't map while reading (no tablet, in transit, etc.), slow down. Don't overconsume. The future-you trying to reconstruct what was read without having mapped is paying triple time.

## Evidence vs. Reference — A Real Distinction

Sung distinguishes evidence and reference more carefully than most learners do, and the distinction earns its keep:

- **Evidence** supports concepts. It's used in arguments, problem-solving, essays, explanations. It connects upward to the conceptual map.
- **Reference** is pure recall info. Names, dates, constants, lookup details. It doesn't change conceptual understanding; you just need to be able to reproduce it.

Both are "store and rehearse" — different storage matters less than different *rehearsal*. Evidence rehearsal is application: write a paragraph using the evidence; teach it as supporting a concept; build a problem around it. Reference rehearsal is bare recall: spaced repetition flash cards.

Conflating them is a common time sink. Treating evidence as reference (flash-carding it) loses the conceptual link. Treating reference as evidence (mapping it) wastes mapping time on info that doesn't earn it.
