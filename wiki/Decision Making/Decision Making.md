# Decision Making

Decision Making is the user's operating layer for choosing under uncertainty without losing time, focus, or adaptability. Use it when the problem is not lack of effort, but too much friction around what to do next, when to commit, when to change course, or how to judge whether a choice was good.

The practical version:

1. Reduce low-value choices with [[wiki/Decision Making/Decisional Delays|Decisional Delays]] and [[wiki/Decision Making/Choice Throttling|Choice Throttling]].
2. Judge decisions by process, not just outcome, with [[wiki/Decision Making/Good Decisions|Good Decisions]].
3. Make uncertain decisions by improving position and expected value with [[wiki/Decision Making/Positional Decisions and Expected Value|Positional Decisions and Expected Value]].
4. Change decisions when new information justifies it with [[wiki/Decision Making/Changing Decisions|Changing Decisions]].

This section is related to [[wiki/Red Team/Red Teaming|Red Teaming]], but it has a different job. Red Teaming tests plans and assumptions. This decision-making layer helps the user move through daily, academic, and personal choices with less drag and better self-regulation.

## Core Idea

Good decisions are not the same thing as good outcomes.

Outcomes contain luck, timing, incomplete information, and changing conditions. The decision process is the part the user can improve. Outcomes still matter, but they should be treated as feedback about the process rather than as the only scorecard.

This matters because many weak decision patterns come from confusing comfort with quality:

- staying with the current option because changing feels costly,
- delaying because more information feels safer,
- overthinking because uncertainty feels intolerable,
- judging a decision only after seeing the result,
- or spending too much focus on choices that do not deserve it.

## Decision Tiers

Not every decision deserves the same amount of thinking.

| Tier | Use For | Method |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Low consequence, low complexity | Daily choices, small preferences, reversible options. | Use [[wiki/Decision Making/Choice Throttling|Choice Throttling]] or a simple yes/no filter. |
| Execution choices | What to do next, when to switch tasks, how to protect focus. | Reduce [[wiki/Decision Making/Decisional Delays|Decisional Delays]] through clearer planning. |
| High uncertainty, meaningful consequence | Study plans, career paths, commitments, major tradeoffs. | Judge process, downside, position, and expected value. |
| Decisions with new information | Changing course, quitting, recommitting, or revising a plan. | Re-evaluate instead of treating consistency as automatically virtuous. |

The mistake is using complex-decision thinking for small choices, or small-choice thinking for complex decisions.

## Operating Loop

Use this loop when a decision matters enough to think about deliberately:

1. Name the decision.
2. Identify the consequence level.
3. Identify the variables creating uncertainty.
4. Decide whether the next move is choice, information-gathering, risk reduction, or delay.
5. Protect the biggest downside.
6. Choose the option that improves position or has better expected value.
7. Record the reason for the decision before the outcome is known.
8. Use the outcome as feedback, not as the sole definition of whether the decision was good.

## Relationship To Learning

Decision making is part of [[wiki/Dimensions/Self-Regulation|Self-Regulation]] and [[wiki/Dimensions/Self-Management|Self-Management]].

It supports learning because study systems are full of small decisions:

- what to study next,
- whether to keep encoding or switch to retrieval,
- whether a bad result means the method failed,
- whether to change a plan,
- how much time to spend collecting information,
- and whether a delay is strategic or avoidant.

The decision-making layer prevents the user from spending cognitive energy on avoidable uncertainty.
