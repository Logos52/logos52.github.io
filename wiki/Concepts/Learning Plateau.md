# Learning Plateau

A learning plateau occurs when repeated practice stops producing meaningful improvement in a skill. It represents the point of diminishing returns where simply doing more of the same yields progressively smaller (or zero) gains.

## Core Thesis

Most plateaus are not caused by reaching a true limit of ability. They are caused by **naive practice** — repeating the same actions while expecting improvement to happen automatically. Breaking through a plateau usually requires changing the *method* of practice rather than increasing its volume.

## Key Mechanisms

**Naive Practice**  
Performing a skill repeatedly without variation, feedback, or deliberate focus on weaknesses. This leads to automation of current (often suboptimal) patterns.

**Method Design Over Volume**  
Progress at higher levels depends more on the quality and design of practice than on hours invested. Good practice typically includes:
- Focused work on fundamentals
- Deliberate pushing beyond current comfort zone
- Introduction of useful variety
- High-quality feedback

**Unconscious Competence as a Trap**  
Once a skill reaches unconscious competence, it becomes smooth and effortless — which is valuable, but it can also make further improvement harder to perceive or pursue without intentional method changes.
