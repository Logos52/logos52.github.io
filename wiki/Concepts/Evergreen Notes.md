# Evergreen Notes

Evergreen notes are production-oriented notes designed to remain useful across long periods of time. They are atomic, concept-focused, densely linked, and written primarily for future reuse rather than immediate consumption.

The fundamental distinction is between **transient capture** (notes that help you remember something you encountered) and **evergreen notes** (notes that help you produce new work later).

## Core Thesis

Good notes are not storage. They are reusable thinking tools. An evergreen note should be distilled enough to be reused, connected enough to resurface when relevant, and synthesized enough to generate new ideas, writing, or decisions.

## Key Principles

**Atomicity**  
Each note should contain one strong idea. Atomic notes travel better across contexts and are easier to recombine.

**Concept Titles**  
Titles should name the idea itself, not just the source ("Signalling Theory" rather than "Notes on Veblen and Status").

**Dense Linking**  
Links are the mechanism by which old thinking becomes available to new problems. The value of a note increases with the number of useful connections it has.

**Production Orientation**  
The test of a good note is whether it can become a paragraph, an argument, a decision, or the seed of a new synthesis. Notes written only for consumption tend to rot.

**Personal Use First**  
Evergreen notes should be written for the specific thinker who will reuse them. Writing for an imaginary future audience often reduces clarity and usefulness.

## Practical Use

When taking notes:
- Push for synthesis rather than transcription.
- Give notes strong, reusable titles.
- Link aggressively to related ideas.
- Revisit old notes regularly (cringe is useful feedback about growth).
- Ask: "What work could this note support in the future?"
