# Bias

Bias is a systematic or preventable error in thinking — a consistent deviation from accuracy or fairness in favor of or against a particular idea, group, or conclusion.

Unlike random error, bias implies a directional distortion that requires active **calibration** to correct.

## Core Thesis

Bias is difficult to detect because the feedback loops that reinforce it are often social. People can remain miscalibrated for long periods when their environment (or social group) consistently rewards the same distorted view.

## Key Mechanisms

**Calibration Problem**  
Thinking without clear external feedback is like shooting a rifle that is not zeroed. The shot group is consistently off, but without an objective target or honest feedback, the shooter never corrects.

**Social Reinforcement**  
Bias is often maintained through mimetic processes: people adopt and reinforce each other’s distorted views, creating closed feedback loops that resist correction.

**Systemic Nature**  
Bias is not usually a one-off mistake. It is embedded in the way questions are framed, evidence is selected, and conclusions are rewarded.

## Practical Relevance

- Central to Red Teaming and critical thinking practices.
- Explains why intelligent people can hold clearly distorted views when surrounded by confirming social environments.
- Requires external calibration mechanisms (diverse models, adversarial review, deliberate exposure to disconfirming evidence).
