# Schelling Point

Also known as a **Focal Point**.

A Schelling Point is a solution that people tend to converge on in the absence of communication or explicit coordination. It is not necessarily the optimal solution on paper, but it stands out because of its prominence, salience, or obviousness in a given context.

Named after economist Thomas Schelling (*The Strategy of Conflict*, 1960).

## Core Thesis

When multiple equilibria exist, people often default to the one that feels most "natural" or noticeable to everyone involved. These points allow coordination without direct communication because they rely on shared cultural knowledge, obvious landmarks, or extreme values.

## Operating Model

```text
Multiple possible solutions exist
-> no direct communication allowed
-> people search for what others are likely to choose
-> converge on the most salient / prominent / obvious option
-> coordination achieved through shared perception rather than agreement
```

## Key Mechanisms

**Salience and Prominence**  
The power of a Schelling Point comes from it being *obviously* special. Examples:
- Two people separated in New York City without phones will often both go to Grand Central Station or Times Square.
- When asked to pick a number between 1 and 100 without communicating, many people pick 1 or 100 (the extremes feel special).
- Meeting times often default to round numbers (7:00, 8:00) rather than 7:17.

**Cultural and Contextual Dependence**  
What counts as a Schelling Point is highly dependent on shared culture and context. A prominent location in one city may mean nothing in another.

**Use in Strategy and Negotiation**  
The side that can successfully define or claim the Schelling Point often gains significant advantage. In negotiations, whoever can make their preferred outcome feel like the "obvious" or "natural" resolution has a structural edge.

## Practical Applications

- **Coordination problems**: Choosing meeting places, times, or signals without communication.
- **Design and branding**: Products or interfaces that become the default choice in their category.
- **Social movements and protests**: Choosing dates, locations, or symbols that feel obvious to participants.
- **Personal strategy**: Understanding that many social defaults (fashion, status signals, career paths) function as Schelling Points.
