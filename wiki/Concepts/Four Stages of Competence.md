# Four Stages of Competence

A model describing the psychological progression of learning a skill. Originally developed in the 1970s, it outlines four stages a learner typically passes through.

## The Four Stages

1. **Unconscious Incompetence**  
   The learner does not know what they don't know. They have wrong intuition and are unaware of their deficiencies.

2. **Conscious Incompetence**  
   The learner becomes aware of their lack of skill. They now have wrong analysis — they know what they're doing wrong but cannot yet do it correctly.

3. **Conscious Competence**  
   The learner can perform the skill correctly, but it requires deliberate thought and effort (right analysis).

4. **Unconscious Competence**  
   The skill has become automatic. The learner performs correctly with right intuition and minimal conscious effort.

## Key Insight

Reaching stages 3 and 4 is significantly harder than reaching stage 2. Traditional education and lesson plans often stop at basic conscious incompetence or early conscious competence. True mastery requires a combination of deliberate practice, experience, and developing accurate intuition.

Even in analytical fields like science and mathematics, high-level performance ultimately depends on well-calibrated intuition.
