# Framing

Framing is the act of presenting information in a way that emphasizes certain aspects while downplaying or omitting others, thereby shaping the emotional response and interpretation of the recipient.

It is one of the most powerful (and often invisible) tools in communication, persuasion, and ideology.

## Core Thesis

How something is framed often matters more than the raw facts. The same information can produce very different reactions depending on whether it is presented as a gain or a loss, a threat or an opportunity, a moral issue or a practical one.

## Key Mechanisms

**Emotional Direction**  
Framing works by attaching positive or negative valence to information. This triggers different cognitive and emotional pathways even when the underlying data is identical.

**Worldview Dependence**  
Effective framing usually aligns with (or exploits) the recipient’s existing mental models and values. It feels natural because it fits what the person already believes.

**Strategic Omission**  
Strong framing rarely lies outright. Instead, it selectively highlights certain elements while rendering others invisible or irrelevant.

## Practical Relevance

- Central to politics, media, marketing, and negotiation.
- A core skill in Red Teaming: being able to reframe a problem from multiple perspectives.
- Closely related to bias — framing is one of the primary ways bias enters decision-making.
