# Unlearning Learning Habits

Unlearning is the active replacement of old habits that no longer serve the learner.

## Summary

Learning a better method is often easier than stopping the old method from taking over. Old learning habits are reinforced by comfort, speed, fluency, and familiar measures of progress. Unlearning requires noticing the old habit, removing its reward, interrupting it, and rehearsing the replacement.

## Why Old Habits Persist

Old learning habits can be fast, low-energy, and emotionally rewarding. A learner may feel productive because they covered pages, wrote many notes, or watched many videos. Those rewards reinforce the behavior even if the durable learning outcome is weak.

This creates a metacognitive problem: the learner may not notice that the success metric is wrong.

## Unlearning Steps

1. Recalibrate reward: stop rewarding comfort, speed, and volume when they do not produce usable knowledge.
2. Identify the old habit: name the trigger, process, and desired outcome.
3. Create a pattern interrupt: define an if-then rule for the trigger.
4. Reflect after practice: ask how the old habit appeared and how the new method improved the outcome.
5. Constraint drill: isolate the bottleneck and practice that smaller piece directly.

## Example

Old habit: when reading dense text, continue passively until the end of the section.

Pattern interrupt: when a dense paragraph appears, stop after one or two paragraphs and create a simplified explanation, analogy, or connection map.
