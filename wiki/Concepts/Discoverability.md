# Discoverability

Discoverability is the degree to which a person can perceive what actions are possible in a given environment or interface, and understand how to perform them.

It sits at the intersection of perception, understanding, and action.

## Core Thesis

Good design makes possible actions visible and comprehensible. Poor design forces users to guess, experiment blindly, or rely on external instructions. Discoverability is not just about aesthetics — it is a fundamental factor in whether something can actually be used effectively.

## Key Mechanisms

**Affordances and Signifiers**  
An object or interface must clearly communicate what it affords (what can be done with it) through visible cues. When these cues are missing or misleading, discoverability collapses.

**Perceived vs. Actual Possibilities**  
What matters is not what actions are technically possible, but what the user can *perceive* as possible based on the information available to them.

**Relationship to Understanding**  
Discoverability is closely linked to understanding. If someone cannot discover how to use something, they cannot form an accurate mental model of how it works.

## Practical Applications

- Physical product design (doors, tools, appliances)
- Software and interface design
- Information architecture
- Physical environments and wayfinding
- Any system where users must figure things out without explicit training
