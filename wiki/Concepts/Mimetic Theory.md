# Mimetic Theory

Mimetic Theory, developed by René Girard, proposes that the majority of human desires are not original or spontaneous. Instead, they are **imitated** (mimetic) — copied from other people.

We want things because we see others wanting them. The object of desire is less important than the model (the person or group) from whom we copy the desire.

## Core Thesis

Most of what we call "our" wants, ambitions, tastes, and even resentments are borrowed. We rarely desire objects or states directly; we desire them because someone else (the model) appears to value them. This creates triangular relationships: Subject → Model → Object.

When many people imitate the same models, competition and conflict intensify around scarce objects or status positions.

## Operating Model

```text
Observe a model (person, group, celebrity, peer)
-> copy their desire for a particular object/status/experience
-> pursue the object
-> if many others copy the same model, rivalry increases
-> conflict or status competition emerges
-> new models appear offering different desires
-> cycle repeats
```

## Key Mechanisms

**Mediated Desire**  
Desire is rarely between a person and an object. It is usually mediated through a third party (the model). Advertising, social media, and status hierarchies work by supplying attractive models.

**Mimetic Escalation**  
When two people desire the same thing because they are imitating each other, the desire intensifies not because the object is inherently more valuable, but because the rivalry itself becomes the driver. This is the root of much social conflict and "keeping up with the Joneses" dynamics.

**Scapegoating**  
When mimetic conflict becomes too intense, groups often resolve tension by uniting against a scapegoat. The victim is blamed for the disorder, even though the real driver was the imitative process itself.

## Practical Relevance

- **Status and ambition**: Many career and lifestyle goals are mimetic rather than authentic. Distinguishing between genuine preference and copied desire is difficult but valuable.
- **Consumerism and trends**: Fashion, tech gadgets, and lifestyle choices spread rapidly through imitation.
- **Conflict and culture**: Political polarization, cancel culture, and culture wars have strong mimetic components.
- **Personal clarity**: Regularly asking "Who am I imitating right now?" is a powerful diagnostic.
