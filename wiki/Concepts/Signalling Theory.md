# Signalling Theory

Signalling theory is a mental model describing forms of communication (often nonverbal) in which the signal itself carries information about the sender's underlying qualities, intentions, or resources.

## Core Thesis

Many behaviors and displays are not direct statements of fact. They are signals whose credibility comes from the cost or difficulty of faking them. The receiver uses the signal to infer something about the sender that would otherwise be hard to observe.

## Types of Signals

**Honest Signals**  
The signal accurately reflects the underlying trait or intention. Hard to fake because of real cost or alignment.

**Dishonest Signals (Bluffing)**  
Signals sent to gain advantage without the corresponding underlying quality. These are vulnerable to detection over time.

**Costly Signals**  
Signals that are expensive or risky to produce. Their credibility comes from the fact that only someone who truly possesses the trait can afford to send them. Classic examples:
- A peacock's tail (Fisherian runaway)
- A human male taking significant risks or generously sharing resources
- Expensive status goods (SUVs, luxury watches) as behavioral versions of peacock feathers

**Virtue Signalling**  
Public expression of morally approved views, often at low personal cost. Frequently criticized when the signal appears to substitute for costly action.

## Key Mechanisms

**Cost as Credibility**  
The more costly or difficult a signal is to fake, the more believable it becomes. This is why "putting skin in the game" is such a powerful signal.

**Runaway Selection**  
When preference genes and signal genes co-evolve, signals can become dramatically exaggerated over generations (e.g., peacock tails).

**Social and Economic Applications**  
Signalling theory explains large parts of:
- Consumer behavior and status goods
- Education and credentials (signalling vs. human capital)
- Charity and philanthropy
- Political and moral speech
- Dating and relationship dynamics
