# Applied Critical Thinking

Applied Critical Thinking is the deliberate use of tools and methods to examine assumptions, arguments, analogies, biases, frames, and alternatives before making decisions.

## Core Idea

The handbook's ACT frame is practical. It is not critical thinking as abstract intelligence. It is critical thinking applied to real decisions under time pressure.

ACT makes the implicit explicit:

- What are we assuming?
- What is the argument?
- What analogy is guiding us?
- What bias may be operating?
- What alternatives have we not explored?
- What does the other actor believe?
- What would make this plan fail?

## Time Pressure

The handbook recognizes that decision makers often do not have unlimited time. Red Teaming does not mean endless analysis. It means training better thinking habits and using structured tools that fit the time available.

The practical rule:

> Slow down enough to avoid cognitive autopilot, then use the smallest tool that improves the decision.

## Cognitive Autopilot

Cognitive autopilot happens when familiar problems trigger familiar answers without fresh examination. It is efficient, but dangerous in complex or novel environments.

Common autopilot patterns:

- "This is just like last time."
- "Everyone knows what this means."
- "We already have a process for that."
- "The expert says it is fine."
- "We do not have time to question assumptions."

## Link To Learning

ACT is a high-value [[wiki/Dimensions/Deep Processing|Deep Processing]] skill. It pushes the learner past recognition and into diagnosis, comparison, explanation, and transfer.

It also fits [[wiki/Techniques/Bear Hunter System|BHS]]:

- Aim: identify what assumptions and questions matter.
- Shoot: investigate evidence and alternative frames.
- Skin: refine the final structure into a clear, testable model.
