# Self-Management

Learning has to survive time, energy, environment, attention, and competing obligations before it becomes executable in real life.

## Summary

Self-Management is the system of time, tasks, habits, attention, energy, environment, and planning that lets the learner actually do the work.

Without Self-Management, good learning techniques remain theoretical. The learner may know what to do but fail to create the conditions required to do it consistently.

## What This Dimension Controls

- Scheduling.
- Task prioritization.
- Focus and attention.
- Environment design.
- Procrastination management.
- Habit formation and habit removal.
- Energy and workload sustainability.
- Whether study plans survive contact with real life.

## Core Moves

- Convert goals into scheduled actions.
- Protect deep work blocks.
- Reduce avoidable friction.
- Identify realistic constraints.
- Track planned time versus actual time.
- Address or mitigate recurring barriers.
- Build routines that reduce dependence on willpower.

## Failure Modes

| Failure | Signal | Repair |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fantasy schedule | The plan repeatedly exceeds actual capacity. | Track actual time and rebuild from reality. |
| Urgency capture | Important learning loses to visible urgent tasks. | Pre-block BHS and SIR sessions. |
| Procrastination loop | Difficult tasks are repeatedly displaced by easier ones. | Reduce startup friction and define the first action. |
| Attention leakage | Sessions exist but are fragmented. | Design focus zones and remove predictable interruptions. |
| Burnout pattern | Output rises briefly, then crashes. | Add recovery, lower scope, and stabilize cadence. |
