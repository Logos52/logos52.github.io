Ali Abdaal makes $3.5 million a year from YouTube.

He has over six and a half million subscribers and one of the most-studied education channels on the internet.

But it's dead.

His most important team-members are quitting and it's clear why. It's breaking because of five mistakes. Each one quiet on its own, but together, they're pulling Ali's channel underwater.

## Mistake 1: He removed the one thing that made strangers click

Look at Ali's best-performing videos.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HFyh-ZlbcAA6Nw0?format=png&name=large)

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HFyiBrAacAI7UvX?format=png&name=large)

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HFyiEpPbYAAZtIm?format=png&name=large)

Every single one puts his authority for those promises EXPLICITLY directly in the title. "1st at Cambridge University" "I made $27k per Week" "156 Words per Minute" Not implied. Stated. The viewer knows exactly why this person has the right to make this claim before they've clicked anything.

Now look at what's being published now.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HFyievQakAAu6WN?format=png&name=large)

The authority signal is gone. And that's a problem, because most viewers landing on these videos are new. They don't know Ali. They have no reason to trust him. And the packaging isn't giving them one.

You can NEVER consistently reach new-viewers without making your perspective explicit. Why should they click on your advice on how to achieve this outcome instead of someone elses? But this is the smallest mistake, and it's far more obvious compared to this next one...

## Mistake 2: His promises are sitting at the wrong rung

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HFyjCX7a4AA1_pl?format=png&name=large)

This is one of the worst titles and thumbnails I've seen in a LONG time. Every promise on YouTube goes through the same cycle.

1. It starts fresh - nobody has made it before, viewers click because they've never seen it claimed.
2. Then other channels copy it.
3. Then the audience gets tired of the obvious version and needs more to justify the click.
4. Then it becomes distrusted entirely - heard too many times, believed by fewer and fewer people.

The question is always: where is your promise on that cycle right now?

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HFyjSTVbMAEvMRG?format=png&name=large)

Take "How to Read More Books." The promise - "get smarter" - is implied, not stated. The mechanism - "reading more books" - is the most familiar one in this entire space. And "How to \[do skill\]" is quite literally the most exhausted format on educational YouTube. Every part of this videos positioning is familiar. There's NO novelty. Nothing to create interest.

Take this video.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HFykNvVaIAAe5M5?format=png&name=large)

It's making the same core promise: "Learn To Read".

However, it's in a far more novel format: "How To \[do skill\] \[better\] Than Everyone Else" The mechanism for achieving this goal is EXPLICITLY stated as: "By reading 3x faster" Same niche. Same audience. But the mechanism carries all the curiosity.

It's clear how it differentiates to what they already know.

There are four rungs to this ladder.

1. Rung one is the bare promise - "make more money," "learn faster." Works only when the promise is new to the space.
2. Rung two adds a constraint - "make money without an audience."
3. Rung three names the mechanism - "learn faster with the Memory Ladder."
4. Rung four makes the promise almost entirely implicit and lets the mechanism carry everything - "Think on Paper."

Ali's recent titles are sitting at rung one and two.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HFyk30CawAADrCY?format=png&name=large)

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HFyk9ZSbwAAZtbV?format=png&name=large)

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HFyk6VOboAA9MJZ?format=png&name=large)

His audience is at rung three and four. Before writing any title, ask: where is this promise on the ladder for this specific audience?

If it's familiar, you need a constraint, a named mechanism, or an implicit reframe. If you can't answer that, the title isn't ready.

## Mistake 3: The packaging makes a promise the video doesn't keep

When someone clicks a video, they're signing a contract.

Not consciously. But the title and thumbnail set specific expectations — how fast the value will arrive, how specific it will be, whether it will feel like something they couldn't find anywhere else.

"How I do blank" implies proprietary access. A process that belongs to this specific person and couldn't have come from anyone else. But what Ali's videos usually deliver is well-structured general advice — advice the viewer has already seen, framed in a way that doesn't require Ali specifically to be the one delivering it.

The contract breaks quietly. The viewer doesn't feel cheated. They just feel like the video didn't quite land. They close the tab slightly earlier than they otherwise would have.

That feeling has a name: expectation misalignment. It's one of the most reliable predictors of poor retention, because it doesn't show up as a single drop-off. It shows up as a gradual drift. The viewer stays, but they're half-present. YouTube reads that drift as dissatisfaction — not dramatically, just consistently — and consistent dissatisfaction is what slowly pulls a video out of distribution.

Every format carries an implicit contract. Before filming, ask: does this video actually deliver what this format promises? If the answer is "mostly" or "kind of," the format is wrong for this content, or the content needs to go deeper. There is no middle ground the viewer won't feel.

## Mistake 5: Thumbnails that agree with the title instead of extending it

The thumbnail and title are two separate levers. They should be doing two different jobs.

The thumbnail delivers the what — the situation, the stakes, the visual proof that something real is happening. The title delivers the how or the why — the mechanism, the frame, the reason this specific video is the one to watch. When both say the same thing, you've wasted one of your two packaging levers before a single viewer has clicked.

Most of Ali's recent thumbnails visualise the title. The text on the thumbnail confirms it. The image illustrates it. The two elements are agreeing with each other rather than extending each other — which means there's no second beat, no additional information, no visual element that opens a gap the title doesn't already open.

But there's a deeper problem. The images people respond to aren't aesthetic choices — they're identity signals. The viewer isn't just asking "is this interesting?" They're asking something more instinctive: does this image belong to my world? Is this for someone like me? Ali's thumbnails aren't answering that question correctly for the audience he's trying to reach.

Before briefing a thumbnail, pull the top-performing videos for your specific promise and mechanism. Look at the thumbnails — not the titles. What emotional expressions appear most often? What symbols? What proof artifacts? Those patterns aren't coincidences. They're the visual language this audience has already shown they respond to. Build from those patterns deliberately. Break one of them intentionally. Never break all of them at once.

## Why these five mistakes are worse together

Ali's channel isn't dying. $3.5 million a year, 6.5 million subscribers, videos still pulling hundreds of thousands of views - that's not collapse.

But the per-video force is softening, and he knows it.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HFymEb4aoAA_xVo?format=jpg&name=large)

These five mistakes don't sit next to each other. They stack.

Each failure makes the others worse. All of them trace back to the same root: a strategy built for an audience that already trusted him, applied to a platform that requires him to earn that trust again with every single video.

The creators who compound on YouTube aren't the ones who work hardest. They're the ones whose model of the audience gets more accurate with every video they post.
